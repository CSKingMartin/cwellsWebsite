Version 1.0 and Rebuilding

June 23, 2016

The good news:

I've finished an initial, maintainable version of my website. I'm taking a break at the moment from code-cleaning to blog (because code-cleaning isn't fun).

I talked a lot with a few people at Connective DX about their own personal websites, and the purpose they had. It was a good conversation -- I feel like entering this field is not something that's by any means traditional. For the most part my general understanding is that when job-hunting or putting your skills out there you need the resume and cover letter.

With the web development industry, and probably many Computer Science fields, your experiences that you would highlight in a resume don't always matter. The field is changing so quickly, that most of the tools and skills associated with them could be irrelevant in the next 5 years.

This makes a personal website extremely valuable. Not only is it your chance to provide a portfolio of work (showing is better than telling), it can itself serve as an example or a hub of examples that showcase new technologies and tools. Proving your adaptability, and grappling with new and undocumented content is likely what hiring companies are looking for. Computer Science is a fluid industry, and we must be able to move with it.

The (kind of) bad news:

I already want to scrap my site and make it leagues better than it is now. Some of these "wants" are purely cosmetic, some functional, but ideally I want to be discovering and creating really really cool stuff, and showing it off. Coworker-Evan was correct in saying that I'll be redesigning my website for my entire life. Really this is awesome. It's exciting and incentive to challenge myself. I want to become, and maintain being, relevant in this workforce.

I think next week my time spent exclusively on my personal development at Connective DX ends, and my time doing a bit of actual work begins. Meanwhile, my idea notebook and my overall journey is just beginning (isn't that quaint and sentimental?). <
